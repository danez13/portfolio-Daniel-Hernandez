collatz conjecture

why?

I had gotten an interest in math as I began at FIU. A major part of this was Numberphile: a relatively small youtube channel that makes amazing math videos and works to visualize different forms of math. Oftentimes the videos are meant to be funny and witty, with an emphasis on complex and diverse math problems. But 2 particular videos had caught my eye, both on the Collatz conjecture. One video meant to serve as an introductory for the Collatz conjecture, explaining the already simple problem and the complications this problem brings. The other video serves as a more visual approach to the problem, emphasizing the structure of the general problem, and its inherent tree-like form.



Analyzing

In order to best analyze the Collatz conjecture, I used various python libraries like matplotlib, pandas, and numpy to develop a sort of rough database, to best get a feel for the problem, and to be able to analyze the conjecture better. I initially stored 3 variables, a value meant to be the starting point of the conjecture, the sequence of numbers the conjecture follows until 1 is reached, and the length of the sequences. All of this would allow me to see a primitive view of the tree-like structure of the conjecture.

What I found was interesting, you could clearly see that as the depth of the tree increased, more and more leaves were present. Another interesting thing I found was that numbers near each other, oftentimes have equal depths, (for example 484,485,and 486 have a depth of 98). Another interesting thing about numbers relative to each other, they tend to have similar sequences with small differences between each other, (for example 484, 485, and 486 each go 6 iterations into the algorithm before reaching 274, and then being in perfect sync with each other).

polar prime plot

Using matplotlib, I made a small representation of the prime number in polar coordinates. For all prime numbers (p) in the set of natural numbers (p,p) is its respective coordinate in the graphical plane. The result was a beautiful interpretation of prime numbers, a rough view of multiplicity with respects to prime numbers.
